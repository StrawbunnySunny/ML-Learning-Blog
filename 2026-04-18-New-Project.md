So, I was doing some work for my searcher and I thought it’d be a fantastic idea if I could develop a predictive model that can estimate the EBITDA of a private company before the CIM is released. Basically, the goal of PE or a search fund (similar to PE but on a smaller scale), is that they want to find these niche deals where the owner maybe hasn’t thought of selling yet (or they are not working with brokers yet), so they can find a profitable business to acquire at a lower price. 

One of the biggest challenges in this process is estimating the EBITDA because it won’t be evident and publicly available. At the same time, if we just did the “spray and pray” method, where we contacted every single business, it would be a massive waste of time, therefore we want to have an idea of which businesses actually fit our target EBITDA range. If we waited for the CIM to be released, then we would be price takers, which affects our competitive edge and our advantage. 

The end goal of my project is to be able to generate a list of companies that fit the target range and then we can automate the organization by running this through an AI model that will rank the companies in order from the best match to the worst (based on location, owner’s age to retirement, EBITDA, etc.) based on weighted features.

However, before I can reach the end goal, it’s imperative that I work on my predictive model first. One of the most challenging things is that private companies do not list their financial information, but one way to get around that would be to train the model on public companies in a similar cap, so think micro-cap, etc. just so it can scale better when the model trains and tests. I would imagine if you trained the model on larger companies, it would be a lot worse at predicting any company in the range of a search fund (SMBs). Another challenge will definitely be sorting the public data and also, it might be hard to test because I don’t have actual private companies and their information to verify if it is correct, however even a model with let’s say a 10% accuracy rate will likely still be able to pull some companies that fall within the range, and then we can manually access from there. Overall, it would still be able to save a fair bit of time even if it cannot work perfectly.
